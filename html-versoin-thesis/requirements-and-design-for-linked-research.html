Management Summary

Research documents on the Web are not as human and machine friendly as they could be. They lack semantic markup and do not use today’s capabilities offered by the World Wide Web to make them more enjoyable and appealing for consumers. Linked Research is an open source project for a semantic content authoring tool, which addresses this problem.

This thesis aims to establish requirements for Linked Research, propose a user interface for the semantic authoring and compare WYSIWYG editors as well as RDFa parsing libraries to evaluate them respectively.

The lack of semantic markup is a major barrier to develop more intelligent document processing on the web. Semantically enriched documents enable more sophisticated ways to flexibly visualize information and create more efficient and effective search interfaces.

But what kind of requirements are imposed on a semantic authoring tool like Linked Research? Which user interface is most suitable for the project’s annotation authoring feature regarding the established requirements? And what kind of WYSIWYG editors as well as RDFa parsing libraries are available on the market to implement Linked Research’s annotation authoring feature? These are the questions that will be approached in this thesis.

The functional and non-functional requirements for Linked Research were established by applying the requirements engineering framework according to Pohl et al. These requirements combined with related work, literature research and meetings with the project leader of Linked Research built the basis to propose the user interface. Relevant WYSIWYG editors and RDFa parsing libraries were gathered and compared based on established criteria to form the basis for a later evaluation.

The main results of this thesis are the following:

  • Established functional and non-functional requirements for Linked Research
  • A proposed user interface for the annotation authoring feature
  • A WYSIWYG editor comparison
  • An RDFa parsing library comparison

Looking forward, the results of this thesis offer the project leader of Linked Research a basis to evaluate a suitable WYSIWYG editor and RDFa library. The established requirements will be integrated into the project’s repository / documentation to facilitate it for other developers to join the open source project.

Introduction

PDF documents are familiar and easy to read for humans. But they are also antithetical to the spirit of the Web, lacking user interactivity and being difficult for machines to read [1] according to “Adventures in Semantic Publishing: Exemplar Semantic Enhancements of a Research Article” by David Shotton et al.

While there have been many efforts in making research publications more accessible and machine friendly through semantic publishing, scholarly communication is predominantly held in data silos like PDF documents or in conventional HTML Web pages with little semantical markup [2]. Here is a brief look at some points in history.

Tim Berners-Lee, working at CERN in 1989, needed an information system that would help scientists to create and discover each other’s documents across systems. In the same year he made a proposal for an information management system; todays World Wide Web [3].

The Web today contains interlinked hypertext documents as proposed in 1990 by him and Robert Cailliau. Their idea of having one-directional hyperlinks between documents and accessing information as a web of nodes [4] has emerged as a large universe of documents, where ideas can be exchanged and knowledge shared between people or institutions.

In 1991 the Camelot Project was launched with the vision to capture documents from any application, send electronic versions of these documents anywhere, and view and print these documents on any machines [5]. By 1992 The Camelot Project eventually developed into PDF, today’s de facto standard when it comes to document formats [6].

PDF and other machine unfriendly formats even dominate in the Web Science community as the community continues to conduct research tracks, workshops, and challenges, in a manner that even the call for papers of top-level Web conferences request research documents that are neither machine nor Web-friendly [7]. Submissions are often solely demanded in Word or PDF formats, where it is challenging to extract information from within [7].

Problem Statement

Through movements like Open Access, data and research publications have become more accessible for the general public. But Sarven Capadisli et al. state in their publication Enabling Accessible Knowledge the following: While there are notable efforts making research publications and data available for free with open access, there is a self-evident disconnect between research documents as far as machines are concerned [8]. According to them, research documents are not as human and machine friendly as they could be, making it unpleasant to us – but also for the machines – to explore research documents on the Web.

As reported by Frank van Harmelen and Dieter Fensel in their publication “Practical Knowledge representation for the Web”, the lack of semantic markup is a major barrier to develop more intelligent document processing on the web [9].

Semantic documents are important because they could create more advanced ways to flexibly visualize information, realize personalization or establish more efficient and effective search interfaces [10].

The open source project called Linked Research, developed by Sarven Capadisli, will enable researchers to take full control, ownership and responsibility of their own knowledge, making it possible to get away from machine unfriendly formats by exploiting the Webs current possibilities to explore hypertext documents in a user-friendly way [11].

Linked Research has not established concrete requirements yet, so that developers can easily contribute to the project. At the moment the project has not implemented an annotation feature to semantically mark words, text passages or paragraphs. To push the project further, an editor component and RDFa parsing library, which fit the requirements of Linked Research, need to be evaluated.

Goal

Linked Research is a semantic content authoring tool (SCA), which makes research knowledge more accessible and publications more pleasant to read for people and machines. It enables authors to edit research papers directly from a Web browser and should empower them to enrich documents with semantic annotations. Consumers of these papers benefit from more interactive and enjoyable publications, while machines can extract more information about those.

The first objective of this thesis is to establish requirements for Linked Research. This facilitates it for developers to join the project. Secondly, a user interface for the semantic annotation should be proposed, since Linked Research does currently not support this feature. Lastly, a comparison of JavaScript editors and RDFa parsing libraries should provide an overview of client sided components to realize the annotation feature.

Background

This section is intended to provide background information and defines terms to understand this thesis.

HTML

The Hypertext Markup Language (HTML) is the core language of the World Wide Web and currently released as version 5.0. The language is used to create web pages and is recommended by the main international standards organization for the World Wide Web, the W3C (World Wide Web Consortium) [12]. HTML specifies the structure of a website with tags. The following listing shows the basic structure of an HTML 5 document:

The DOM (Document Object Model)

The DOM is a language neutral interface, which allows scripts to dynamically access and change the structure of an (HTML)-document. With the DOM it is possible for scripting languages like JavaScript to access HTML elements and rearrange, delete or add new elements to the HTML-document. The DOM makes DHTML (Dynamic HTML) possible [14].

JavaScript

JavaScript is a dynamic programming language and mostly used for web pages. It is often used in web browsers (client side) to interact with users and dynamically change document content that is displayed [15].

Web Browsers

Web browsers (or just browsers) are used to represent web pages on the World Wide Web. They provide the user interface for web applications. The current major browsers on the market are Firefox, Internet Explorer, Google Chrome, Opera and Safari with their corresponding web browser engines Blink (Google Chrome and Opera) Trident (Internet Explorer), Gecko (Firefox) and Webkit (Safari). The web browser engine is a software component that is responsible to display content on the screen [16].

What you see is What you…

WYSIWYG

WYSIWYG stands for “What-You-See-Is-What-You-Get” and describes an editing system, in which a user’s edited content is displayed in a form close to the final representation of the text, while he is writing the text [17].

WYSIWYM

WYSIWYM is an acronym for “What-You-See-Is-What-You-Mean”. A WYSIWYM editor tries to capture the separation of presentation and content when writing a document. The focus of a WYSIWYM-editor lies on the preservation of the meaning of an element within a text instead the presentation of the element in the document [17].

Closely related to WYSIWYM is the term semantic authoring / publishing. This term is described as follows by [17]: Semantic authoring aims to add more meaning to digitally published documents. If users do not only publish the content, but at the same time describe what it is they are publishing, then they have to adopt a structured approach to authoring. The WYSIWYM concept forms the basis for this approach.

Linked Data

Linked Data is part of the Semantic Web and describes free available structured data reachable through Uniform Resource Identifiers (URI). It is published in a format (often RDF) so that it can be interlinked with other data, and through that, be more useful [18].

Time Berners-Lee published on 2006-07-27 an article called „Linked Data – Design Issues“. In this publication Berners-Lee addresses four rules for Linked Data [18]:

  1. Use URI as names for things.
  2. Use HTTP URIs so that people can look up those names.
  3. When someone looks up a URI, provide useful information, using the standards (RDF, SPARQL).
  4. Include links to other URIs. So that they can discover more things.

HTTP URI

URIs build the basis of Linked Data and are used as names for things [18]. They are a sequence of characters to identify an abstract or physical resource on the Web [19]. The HTTP scheme is the most common used form of URIs and is used since 1990 by the World-Wide Web global information initiative [20]. They provide the user a simple way to explore the Web and its documents (resources).

RDF

RDF is a resource description framework to express information about resources. The framework is specified by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) and lets anyone make any statement about anything [21]. A statement is composed of three atomic parts (subject, predicate and object). Those three parts together are called a triple. The subject (line 1 of listing 2) says what the statement is about and the predicate (line 2 of listing 2) describes the relationship to its object (line 3 of listing 2) resource. Typically, triples are described with HTTP URIs [22]. The following triple statement gives an example:

The statement from above says that the thing <http://renatostauffer.ch/i#> knows the thing <http://csarven.cs/#i> . Those resources (things) can now be further described through RDF vocabularies, which provide semantic information about these resources [23]. As the example from above shows, predicates are vocabulary terms that are used to relate the subject with an object. The predicate itself is an HTTP URI, which has a description for the term in use when dereferenced.

RDFa

Resource Description Framework in Attributes or simply RDFa is a W3C recommendation that enables authors of HTML, XHTML and other XML-based document types to enrich their documents with metadata. RDFa provides a set of attributes to describe elements in XML languages [24]:

about
Defines what the resource is about (subject in a triple statement) [25].
rel / rev
Describes the relationships between resources (predicate in a triple statement) [26].
src / href / resource
Those three attributes are used to state the object of a triple statement. While the resource-attribute is intended to state a non-navigable partner resource of a subject [27], the href-attribute is used in conjunction with navigable resources [28]. The src-attribute is used, when a resource is embedded [29].

SPARQL

SPARQL (SPARQL Protocol and RDF Query Language) is a query language for RDF data and can be used to query RDF triple statements across different data sources [30].

Related Work

The idea of having a publishing tool paired with a annotation feature is nothing new. Different products, with different focuses have emerged. Three of those products are RDFaCE, Loomp and Wordlift, which will be introduced in the following sections. RDFaCE has been chosen because the focus of this product lies on usability, which is a major requirement of Linked Research (see section 5.2.2 Non-functional Requirements). The other two tool’s main focus lies on simple solutions concerning the semantic enrichment of documents by non-domain experts. Linked Research focuses also on accessibility, (see section 5.2.2 Non-functional Requirements) which makes loomp and Wordlift good candidates to showcase related work.

RDFaCE

RDFaCE is a semantic content authoring tool, which integrates four different views on the semantically annotated content. Those views are synchronized, which means that when a change in a view happens, the other views apply the change. The focus of RDFaCE lies on usability by implementing the four views for different personas involved in the content creation life-cycle [31].

The four views RDFaCE implements are [31]:

WYSIWYG View
Used to create text by authors, journalists etc.
WYSIWYM View
Extends the WYSIWYG View.
RDF Triple View
Useful for curators and authors for verifying that entities and facts were correctly annotated.
Source Code View
Shows the HTML source code of the article and its RDFa annotations. This is useful for software and knowledge engineers.

Loomp

Loomp is a SCA with a one click annotation strategy (OCA) according to “One Click Annotation” by Heese et al. [32]. Loomp addresses the issue for missing tools to create semantic content by non-domain experts. The main focus lies on simplicity by providing an intuitive interface. The editor allows users to annotate words or phrases with references to ontologies and to create relationships between annotated phrases. Loomp relies on a server to handle and process the semantic content [33].

Wordlift

Wordlift is a semantic web plugin for the content management system (CMS) WordPress. The user is able to run a semantic web analysis on an authored text to automatically find entities (resources) from the Linked Open Data Cloud, that do match words or phrases within the text. After an entity has been found, the user can bind it to the text with a few simple clicks [34].

Requirement Engineering

In this section, we will derive functional and non-functional requirements for Linked Research. According to Sommerville, functional requirements are services the system should provide. They determine how the system should react to inputs and how it should behave in certain situations [36, p. 119]. On the other hand, non-functional requirements define constraints on the functionality offered by the system. They often apply to the whole system and relate to system properties such as usability, reliability or portability [36, p. 119 -122].

In this thesis the requirements engineering framework according to Pohl et al. [35] is used as a reference to establish the requirements. The used framework is structured as shown in figure 1.

The framework is composed of three parts: The System Context, Core activities and Requirements artefact.

Firstly the System Context with its four facets (Subject-, Usage-, IT System- and Development facet) will be described to identify important characteristics of the system and give the reader of this thesis an overview of the system’s context. Depending on the established context, goals were determined and scenarios to be implemented are worked out. Those requirement artefacts are documented as functional and non-functional requirements (see Appendix) and scenarios (see 5.4 Scenarios).

The management and validation of the requirements are not addressed in this thesis.

Each software-intensive system lies within a certain context and is through that influenced by the definition of the system requirements [35]. The Usage Facet describes how the user interacts with the system [35]. In the case of Linked Research the Usage Facet is described with the UML 2.0:

Figure 2 describes the two stakeholders (author and consumer) and how they interact with Linked Research. If the term user or users is used in this thesis, both stakeholders (author and user) are addressed. From a user’s perspective the following use cases arise:

Edit Text
This is the most basic use case for Linked Research. The author creates or edits text directly in the browser.
Add Triple Statement
The author can enrich the written text with RDF triples via a triple authoring tool. For this to be working, the user must be able to define a subject, predicate and object of a triple.
Triple Creation Support
Linked Research may support authors to add triple statements by providing suggestions for predicates or objects in the triple creation process. This is useful for non-domain experts and used by other SCA tools like RDFaCE.
Remove Triple Statement
The author can remove added triple statements from the document.
View Document Graph
A user can view the document and its triple statements as a visualized graph. This enables the author to quickly review triple statements and their relation. Consumers obtain the possibility to explore the document in a visual way and figure out how sections of the document are related to each other or external resources.
Rearrange Table of Content
A user can rearrange the table of content. This will automatically update the sections of the document.
Offline and Local Storage
The text the author is editing gets stored locally. This way, off-line editing is possible.
Export Document
The user is able to export the document in HTML format or print the document (PDF format).
Section Identification
Users can identify sections by hovering over paragraphs and text passages. The user can apply different views for the document without changing the underlaying structure of the DOM. The following views are provided: LNCS, ACM, W3C-REC and Native.

Subject Facet

After the establishment of the Usage Facet, the Subject Facet is determined. This facet describes what kind of objects must be represented by the system [35]. Table 3 describes objects within the System Context that are important for Linked Research.

Important objects within Linked Research
Object Description
RDF Subject An RDF Subject represents some part of the document the author writes. This can either be a single or multiple words, a single or multiple paragraphs or the hole document itself.
RDF Predicate An RDF predicate is the second part of a triple statement and defines the property for the RDF Subject. A predicate must always be an URI.
RDF Object An RDF object is the third part of an RDF statement an author creates. An object can either be an URI or a text literal.
RDF Triple Statement A triple statement is composed of an RDF Subject, RDF Predicate and RDF Object. Triple statements are stored within an RDF triple store (see next row).
TripleStore An RDF triple store, persists RDF statements. Triple statements can be queried, inserted or deleted using the TripleStore object.
TripleEditor object An object responsible to create or remove triple statements has to be provided.
DOMManipulator If a new triple statement gets created, this change needs to be reflected in the DOM with RDFa. The DOMManipulator is responsible to add or remove RDFa from elements within the DOM
SearchHandler An object to handle API calls to a service that provides access to different RDF vocabularies must be available. This enables the author to search for existing vocabularies.
Editor An editor object to handle text authoring must be provided. This object may also be responsible to show the UI for the TripleEditor.

IT System Facet

The IT System Facet contains all aspects concerning the operational and technical environment oft the system. This also includes restrictions or guidelines [35]. The online documentation of Linked Research defines some design principles, which need to be taken into account when defining the requirements of Linked Research. This includes:

  • Documents are human and machine friendly [38].
  • Using the plain old semantic HTML marking process, with further semantic annotations using microformats and RDF [38].
  • No server required. Works on local machine [38].
  • No installation. No account creation [38].
  • A Web browser is the only requirement, whether it is a Line Mode Browser, or Firefox Nightly [38].
  • Licenses and rights: Apache License and CC0 [38].

The Development Facet contains all aspects regarding the development process of the system including guidelines, constraints and development tools or quality assurance methods [35].

This approach tries to reuse existing software and frameworks as much as possible and thus reduces development time for a much faster delivery. Github (a versioning tool) is used to manage the code base of Linked Research and prototypes.

The publication Enabling Accessible Knowledge defines in the section Conceptual Design design principles, to which Linked Research sticks to [39]. Those are:

The Rule of Least Power
This means that when developing for the Web, the least powerful language should be chosen. Capadisli et al. state in Enabling Accessible Knowledge the following: By design, HTML fulfils the principle of least power. As far as computer languages and their environments go, HTML is the least common denominator; it can be used virtually from any device; from command-line terminals, desktop browsers, smartwatches to cars, whether on or offline. Together with HTTP, the undeniable penetration of HTML, both on a technological and social level makes it a clear winner for information distribution [39].
RFC 1958
This Request for Comments (RFC) says, that: the principle of constant change is perhaps the only principle of the Internet that should survive indefinitely [40].

Linked Research should base its design decisions around these design principles for maximizing the potential for knowledge acquisition of scholarly publications [39].

A structured natural language approach by using a standard form adapted from Sommerville [36, p. 131-135] is used to record the requirements. This has several advantages:

  • On one hand it keeps the expressiveness and understandability of natural language and on the other hand it ensures that there is some degree of uniformity [36, p. 131].

  • The structure of the forms define which information is important [37, p. 267].
  • The same information is at the same place. This ensures comparability and a selective access to the requirements [37, p. 267 - 268].

Comparison Editor

This section describes, how different WYSIWYG editors got compared to form a basis for a later evaluation for the editor component.

Text Editing on the Web

Before considering a particular editor, known approaches to edit text within the browser need to be determined.

A search for the terms approaches wysiwyg editors and text editing approaches on web, led to the following Wikipedia page: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Online_rich-text_editor. This source is not scientific enough for the thesis, but offers new insights on what kind of search terms could be used to get more relevant material. The consultation of the page led to the following new search terms: contentEditable, alternatives for contentEditable, designMode, textarea, inline editing wysiwyg, iframe wysiwyg editor. Searches on Google.com for these terms resulted in the following approaches to edit text on the Web:

Inline editing
Inline editing is described as follows by [42]:
Inline edit is a design pattern that enables the user to edit text directly in the page without requiring going to a separate page or opening a dialog overlay. It makes the interaction more direct and intuitive as the user can edit the text in the same place where it is shown.

A similar definition is used in the documentation of the CKEditor, a well known WYSIWYG editor [43].

Framed editing

Framed editing, also called classic editing, is described by [44] as follows:

In this usage scenario the editor is most often represented by a toolbar and an editing area placed in a specific position on the page, usually as a part of a form that you use to submit some content to the server. Sometimes it is also called “framed editing”, because in this case the editor creates a temporary iframe element for itself.

From a more technical point of view, inline editing is enabled through the use of the following techniques:

designMode
The designMode attribute of the document object is able to make a whole HTML-page editable by just setting it to true [45].
contentEditable
In contrast to the designMode, the contentEditable attribute makes just some part of the document (like a div-element) editable [46]. Listing 3 shows how the attribute can be used:

The <div>-element down below is now editable due to setting the <contentEditable>-element equal to true (pleas visit http://renatostauffer.ch/requirements-engineering-for-linked-research#text-editing to try it out).

You can edit this div element. Try it…

After determining different approaches, a Web research was made to see what kind of authoring on the Web (inline or framed) offers the best editing experience, with Linked Research’s established requirements in mind. Searches on Google.com with the search terms in-place editing user experience wysiwyg, inline editing user experience wysiwyg, WYSIWYG inline vs framed editing, WYSIWYG framed editing user experience and inplace editing user experience wysiwyg resulted in the following:

As far as it comes to editing experience for WYSIWYG editors, inline editing (also called in-place editing or inline edit) is emerging. This is shown by [47], as they compiled blog posts from different sources and show the discussion around this approach.

Also, big Content Management Systems like Drupal picked up on this development. Their Drupal distribution Spark aimed to work out solutions for authoring experience problems. This included the implementation of an inline WYSIWYG editor [48].

Other services like Medium.com claim to be the best writing tool on the Web by applying the inline editing strategy and stripping out a lot of formatting features other editors offer [49]. Jeff Eaton, senior digital strategist at Lullabot, sees potential for focused web sites like Medium.com by applying this strategy [50].

For Linked Research, the inline editing approach is a suitable option too, as its formatting is also very focused (see requirement Text formatting F-4 in the appendix). This enables the author to concentrate on the actual content of his / her publication.

The challenge is to find an inline WYSIWYG editor that matches the established requirements. Especially the requirements Edit text (F-1), Text formatting (F-4), Enrich DOM (F-13), Add new Paragraph (F-16), Add new Section (F-17) and The DOM needs to keep its semantical representation (F-18) need to be considered when evaluating a possible editor (see Appendix).

The following categories including different criteria for the comparison were established:
General information
This category includes general information about the editors.
Support
“Support” looks if there is a community around the editor and if there is documentation available. As stated by [51], open source projects often lack good documentation. Thus this is an important attribute when considering the editor.
Requirement Fulfillment
This category determines if the editor fulfills the established requirements.
Extensibility
To make it possible for Linked Research to work, new functionality has to be added to the editor. This category looks if the editor can easily be extended with custom buttons and events attached to it.
Compatibility
This checks how well an editor handles different browser versions.

Summary and Outlook

This section summaries the thesis and gives an outlook for future processing concerning Linked Research and the results of this thesis.

Summary

Due to the study of existing Linked Research documentation, meetings with Sarven Capadisli, the consultation of related work and research on requirements for SCA tools, the System Context for Linked Research was established. This helped to understand the system with its environment and provided a basis to establish concrete functional and non-functional requirements. These requirements are recorded using a form and structured natural language.

Important objects for the implementation have been discovered and a possible user interface for Linked Research’s annotation editor has been proposed using mockups.

Editors and RDFa parser libraries have been compared. Tables 7 – 14 show the result of these comparisons. The code examples on github serve as showcases for the different libraries and can be used as a starting point when implementing the components.

Therefore the goals for this thesis have been reached.

Outlook

The requirements are now open to be transformed to the repository or the documentation of Linked Research. Sarven Capadisli can now evaluate a suitable editor and RDFa parser component based on the established tables in section 6 Comparison Editor and 7 Comparison RDFa Libraries.

Although the focus concerning non-functional requirements lies on usability, accessibility and evolvability, Linked Research has not established requirements concerning other non-functional properties like efficiency or reliability yet. Those are open to be defined. Further, the use cases View Document Graph and Offline and Local Storage have not been addressed too much throughout the thesis. Hence they need to be specified in more detail.
